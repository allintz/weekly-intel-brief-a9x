What’s Surprising

Analysis — April 2026

  • The Kokotajlo/Lifland update is the most striking: moving the Automated Coder median by 18 months (~mid-2028 from late 2029) in a single quarterly update reflects a 4-month capability doubling time. At that rate, another quarterly update in July could put the median in early 2028 or late 2027.
  • The convergence between Metaculus Q5121 (Feb 2028, very high bar) and the Automated Coder milestone (mid-2028) is unusual — these are normally years apart. Either Q5121 has an unusually low resolution bar in practice, or capability development is compressing the gap between functional milestones and formal definitions.
  • Greenblatt’s alignment concern (“Current AIs seem pretty misaligned to me”) is landing at the same moment his capability timeline is moving up. This is the core scenario alignment researchers worried about: not a slow gradual transition, but rapid capability gains outpacing alignment work.
  • Anthropic is simultaneously briefing the government on a withheld cybersecurity AI (Mythos), declining a $800B+ IPO, and watching its relationship with the administration thaw. These three signals together suggest the company is in a genuinely novel strategic position that no governance framework was designed for.

Implications for Our Work

The compressing timeline between named capability milestones (Automated Coder mid-2028) and the formal AGI bar (Metaculus Feb 2028) creates acute urgency for governance work. The practical question is whether governance institutions can move at the pace of 4-month capability doublings. The EA Forum post on Anthropic’s IPO is directly relevant: if Anthropic goes public under shareholder pressure before sufficient governance frameworks exist, the mission structure that distinguishes it from OpenAI is at risk. The Mythos situation — a withheld cybersecurity model being briefed to governments — is a preview of the dual-use problem at scale: capabilities exist before the frameworks to govern them.

Bright Line Watch — Methodology & Trajectory Deep Dive

What BLW Is

brightlinewatch.org

Bright Line Watch is a bipartisan group of political scientists at US universities who survey experts and the public on democratic practice in the US. Founded in 2017 in response to concerns about democratic backsliding. Conducts ~4 waves per year. The academic team includes researchers from Princeton, Rochester, Dartmouth, Chicago, and other institutions.

The 0–100 Scale

BLW surveys respondents on ~30 specific democratic performance measures grouped into categories: elections, rights, constraints on executive, rule of law, civil liberties. Scores are expert assessments of performance, not predictions or market probabilities. The aggregate is roughly comparable to V-Dem’s Liberal Democracy Index but more US-specific and more frequently updated.

Historical Trajectory

Summer 2018 (~73): Baseline modern era, early norm erosion noted — Winter 2020–21 (~62): Post-Jan 6 drop; peaceful transfer questions enter survey — Fall 2022 (~58): Sustained decline; election administration category under pressure — Winter 2024–25 (~48): Sharpest single-wave drop in survey history coinciding with second Trump term inauguration — Spring 2026 (~44, provisional): Continued decline on executive overreach, press freedom, and independent judiciary categories.

Spring 2026 Wave: Key Findings

Provisional spring 2026 wave (full report pending): Executive constraints category scored lowest in survey history. Press freedom category declined for sixth consecutive wave. Positive: electoral administration scores held steady, suggesting resilience in election machinery itself even as surrounding institutional constraints weakened.

What BLW Measures Well

  • Expert consensus on norm erosion vs. public perception of norm erosion
  • Granular breakdown by democratic dimension (not just a single index)
  • Longitudinal consistency — same items over time allow genuine trend detection

What BLW Doesn’t Capture Well

  • Real-time developments — survey waves are months apart
  • Sub-national democratic variation (state vs. federal)
  • Economic drivers of democratic support or erosion
  • International comparison context (it’s US-focused)
