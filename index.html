Expert Timeline Comparison — AC vs. AGI gaps

Where each forecaster places Automated Coder (diamond) and full AGI (circle), with forecast date
What’s surprising here:
  • Kokotajlo & Lifland both see a ~2yr gap between AC and AGI. This is a specific bet that software engineering automates before other domains (robotics, physical tool use, long-horizon planning, scientific research). If you think coding is the hardest cognitive task, this is wrong; if you think it’s the easiest to automate because it has clean feedback signals, this is right.
  • The Metaculus community median (Feb 2028) for strict AGI is between Kokotajlo’s AC and AGI. Metaculus’ definition includes physical robotic assembly of a Ferrari model. The community thinks that doesn’t add much delay beyond AC — Kokotajlo thinks it adds ~2 years. This is a real disagreement about whether robotics is the bottleneck.
  • Greenblatt’s full distribution (Apr 2026 update). P(full AI R&D automation): 7% by EOY 2026, 19% by EOY 2027, ~30% by EOY 2028, 54% by EOY 2031 (approximate median). So his median is ~2031 — closer to Kokotajlo’s AGI point than to his often-quoted 2028 probability. He also notes his views are “not that reflectively stable” and updated “a moderate amount toward longer timelines after thinking about it.”
  • Cotra’s 15% by 2030 is the most spread-out distribution — she assigns significant mass to the 2040s+. The community and AI Futures Project are much more concentrated.
  • Forecast recency matters. Kokotajlo/Lifland updated in Q1 2026 (very recent, post-Opus 4.6). Cotra’s last public update was 2024 — her estimate would likely shift sooner if she republished today given the 2025–2026 capability jumps.
Other ways to visualize this: Each has tradeoffs.
  • CDF curves (like Cotra’s original paper): Shows each forecaster’s full probability distribution over time. Most information-rich, but harder to read at a glance and requires each forecaster to have published a distribution (most haven’t).
  • Confidence interval bars (10%–90% ranges with median marker): Surfaces uncertainty explicitly. Works when forecasters publish ranges; for point estimates we’d have to impute.
  • Update-history chart: Show how each forecaster has moved over time (Kokotajlo shifted 1.5yr sooner between late 2025 and Q1 2026). Surfaces velocity of belief updates, but requires historical snapshots.
  • Heatmap: Forecasters on Y-axis, years on X-axis, cells colored by probability mass. Very dense, good for researchers; confusing for casual readers.
Happy to build any of these if useful.
